The US Politician Network

We extracted 6,138 American politicians from Wikipedia, mapped the links between them, and read every word of every article. This is what we found about who is connected, and what they say.

01 — The Idea

Who is connected, and what do they say?

Wikipedia is more than an encyclopaedia — it is a graph. Every politician's article links to colleagues, rivals, mentors, and successors. Taken together, these links form a network that quietly encodes a community's structure.

We treat each US politician as a node, and each cross-link between two of their Wikipedia pages as an edge. Then we read the articles themselves. The result is a dataset that lets us ask two parallel questions:

  • AWho is connected? — what does the link structure look like, and does it sort itself by party, region, or era?
  • BWhat do they say? — how does language in the articles differ across communities and parties?

The interesting bit is whether those two answers agree. If communities found purely from link structure also show up in the language, then Wikipedia encodes the same political groupings twice — once in its graph, once in its words.

Research questions

  1. Do politicians cluster into communities in the network?
  2. Do those communities correspond to party or ideology?
  3. Are central nodes more influential?
  4. Does language similarity align with network connections?
  5. Is Wikipedia actually neutral — do parties get described differently?

02 — The Data

From Wikidata to a clean corpus

We assembled the dataset in two passes. First, we used the Wikidata SPARQL endpoint to find the most cross-wiki-covered US politicians as seeds. Then we walked the Wikipedia link graph out from those seeds to capture their connections.

Pipeline

  1. SPARQL seed query — rank politicians by number of Wikipedia language editions covering them; take the top 800.
  2. Link walk — for each seed's Wikipedia page, follow internal links and keep the ones that point to other US politicians.
  3. Filter — humans, US-office holders, born before 1990, died after 1980 (so we focus on the modern era).
  4. Article fetch — for every politician, pull the full plain-text Wikipedia article via the extracts API, strip the footer sections (References, See also, External links, Bibliography …).
  5. Tokenisation — sentence-split, lowercase, remove stopwords, lemmatise with WordNet, detect significant bigrams via χ2.

03 — The Network

Structure of the graph

Each node is a politician. Each edge connects two whose Wikipedia pages link to each other. What does the resulting graph look like — is it dense, scale-free, fragmented?

A few hubs, a long tail

Most politicians sit on the edge of the network with only a handful of links — the median politician has just 5 connections. But a small set of figures act as hubs, with the most-linked reaching around 1,000 connections. The average is pulled up to 11 by this heavy tail.

The contrast with a random network of the same size is stark. In a random graph everyone has roughly the same number of connections, clustered tightly around the average. The real network is far more unequal — the signature of a system where a few prominent politicians (presidents, leaders, long-serving senators) are referenced far more than everyone else.

At a glance

  • 6,049 politicians in the connected network
  • 33,030 links between them
  • 5 connections for the median politician
  • ~1,000 for the most-connected
  • One giant component — almost no isolated politicians

Who links to whom?

Beyond raw connection counts, we can ask whether politicians preferentially link to others like themselves. The assortativity coefficient measures this: positive means similar politicians cluster together, near-zero means the attribute is unrelated to the link structure.

The headline: party is the single strongest predictor of who connects to whom in the network. This is not obvious in advance — Wikipedia's links are written by thousands of independent editors with no coordination, yet the resulting graph still sorts itself substantially along party lines.

04 — The Text

What 56 million characters of biography reveal

For every politician we extracted their full Wikipedia article — intro, career, positions, the lot — and stripped out boilerplate footer sections (References, See also, External links, Bibliography …). The result is a clean text corpus of about 56 million characters.

Our first question of the text: is Wikipedia actually neutral? We scored every article with VADER, a sentiment tool that rates text from −1 (very negative) to +1 (very positive), and looked at how the scores break down.

Wikipedia leans positive

So the answer to "is Wikipedia neutral?" is, on average, no — it is gently flattering. Politicians are described in more positive than negative terms. That raises an obvious follow-up: is the positivity spread evenly, or do some groups get warmer treatment than others?

But it is even-handed between parties

Gender tells a slightly different story: articles about female politicians are marginally more positive than those about male politicians, and the difference is statistically significant (Mann–Whitney p = 0.002) — though the effect is small.

Seniority buys better press

How we prepared the text

We used the MediaWiki extracts API with explaintext=1 to get clean plaintext — no HTML, no infoboxes, no tables, no [1] citation markers, no images. Section markers == Section == were preserved so we could strip out boilerplate footer sections (References, See also, External links, Bibliography …) along with their subsections.

For the deeper text analysis we then tokenise: lowercasing, stopword removal, and WordNet lemmatisation, plus a multi-word-expression detector for collocations like "supreme court" or "vice president".

Still to come

TF–IDF word clouds. We are finishing an analysis of the most distinctive vocabulary per party and per community — which words set Democrats apart from Republicans, and whether the link-structure communities also differ in language. Those visualisations will land here soon.

05 — Communities

Do the parties hide in plain sight?

Run a community detection algorithm that knows nothing about parties or ideology — just the link structure. Then colour the resulting communities by what we know. If the algorithm independently rediscovers Democrat / Republican … that says something.

Not party lines — prominence and geography

If you had to guess, you might expect the communities to split neatly into Democrats and Republicans. They don't. The median community is only 56% one party, and just 4 of the 33 are more than 70% one-sided. So while party predicts individual links (as we saw with assortativity), it does not cleanly carve up the network into red and blue blocs.

Instead, two other forces shape the communities:

  • 1National prominence. The three largest communities are dominated by presidents, vice presidents and national figures — George W. Bush, Bill Clinton and Reagan in one; McCain, Romney and Harris in another; Trump, Biden and Pelosi in a third. These clusters are party-mixed, because national politicians of both parties reference each other constantly.
  • 2State and regional politics. The smaller communities are strikingly geographic: a New York City cluster (Giuliani, Bloomberg, Cuomo), an Illinois cluster (Obama, Jesse Jackson, Durbin), a New Jersey cluster (Christie, Corzine, Menendez), an Alaska cluster (Palin, Stevens, Young). Politicians who govern the same place link to each other regardless of party.

So the network's communities are organised less by ideology than by where you operated and how prominent you were — a reminder that Wikipedia's link structure tracks shared context and co-occurrence in events, not political alignment.

Coming soon — naming the communities

We are finishing a TF–IDF analysis that surfaces the most distinctive words in each community's articles. That will let us put a label on each cluster from its language alone, and check whether the link-based communities also stand apart in what is written about them.
